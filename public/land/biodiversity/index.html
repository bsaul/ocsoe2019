State of the Birds

Long-running local study of bird populations highlights changes to our local environment and illustrates the global connectedness of the natural world.

Each May and June, after an exhausting migration for many bird species, a group of volunteers travel the roads of Orange County to listen for who has chosen to make a nest in our neighborhood. The mini bird breeding survey, a regional version of the long-running national survey, has tracked local bird populations since 1999. Such systematicaslly collected data makes it possible to examine trends in bird biodiversity on a local scale. The following table shows species identified as of local concern by the New Hope Audubon Society.

Certain iconic specialist species such as the Wood Thrush have declined by as much as 5.5% annually over the last 20 years. Anyone who has enjoyed a summer hike in one of our local parks will certainly recognize the Wood Thrush’s flutely song. While loss and fragmentation of the eastern deciduous forest has certainly contribute to Wood Thrush decline, the winter range and travel routes of migratory birds are equally important, demonstrating how changes to a local ecosystem can have global consequences.

Recommended Actions

Actions for BOCC

  • Install bird proofing on windows of county buildings
  • Encourage use of native plants in landscaping and reduce amount of lawn/mown areas.

Actions for citizens

  • Get involved with science: MBBS, New Hope Audubon, Chapel Hill Bird Club, inaturalist
  • Plant native trees: https://projects.ncsu.edu/goingnative/howto/index.html
